Japan's J-power plans to expand wind energy
By Yuka Obayashi — October 31, 2025

Japan's Electric Power Development Company, known as J-Power, announced that it will close two old coal-fired power plants by March 2029. This move supports Japan's plan to cut carbon dioxide emissions by 46% by 2030, compared with 2013 levels. The company said J-Power is also focusing on renewable energy, especially offshore wind power. J-Power still depends heavily on coal, which provides more than half of its electricity sales in Japan. To meet future energy and environmental goals, J-Power plans to close other inefficient coal plants. Closing all these units would reduce the company's total power generation capacity by 12%. Japan's overall electricity demand is expected to increase in the coming years because of the growth of data centers and semiconductor factories. These industries require large amounts of power, reversing Japan's previous trend of falling electricity use. The company emphasized that Japan must expand renewable energy sources, like wind power, to achieve both carbon neutrality (zero net emissions) and energy security (a stable power supply). However, offshore wind developers are facing challenges such as rising material, equipment, and labor costs.

📰 النص الأصلي — BBC

Jonathan O'Callaghan — 01 November 2025

Harvesting solar energy in orbit and transmitting it to Earth has long been an idea discussed by scientists. Now, several companies believe they are close to making it possible. In March this year, an unusual experiment took place at an American football stadium in Florida. Instead of footballs being thrown, beams of concentrated light were projected across the field of a stadium. These beams, lasting a few minutes, were directed from one side and captured on a screen on the other. The light, collected from the Sun, was projected using large 1.2-metre lenses positioned on the field, functioning like magnifying glasses. During the test, they successfully transmitted 100 watts of energy over a distance of about 105 metres.

Star Catcher is one among several companies around the world working on space-based solar power technologies, a concept that has remained uncertain for decades. The principle is to provide Earth with clean, abundant energy by capturing sunlight in space and transmitting it to the ground or to other satellites. Unlike solar panels on Earth, which are limited by weather, atmospheric filtering, and the day-night cycle, solar panels in space can collect sunlight almost continuously and at much higher efficiency. This approach offers a power density more than ten times greater than terrestrial systems, allowing for a much higher conversion of solar energy into electricity.

Space-based solar power operates on the same principle as terrestrial systems — converting sunlight into electricity — but with a crucial difference: the panels are positioned above the Earth's atmosphere. This means they can collect sunlight unfiltered by clouds or gases, which typically reflect about 30% and absorb around 25% of the Sun's energy before it reaches the surface.

Bringing such a concept to life poses significant challenges. Building large satellite constellations would be complex, potentially controversial, and would require multiple rocket launches. Moreover, other renewable energy sources are cheaper and quicker to deploy, which is crucial in addressing global warming and reducing dependence on fossil fuels. Despite these obstacles, advocates believe the benefits of constructing solar power stations in space will eventually outweigh the costs.

📝 نص الفقرة الأصلي مع الفراغات

EcoWave Technologies is an emerging company in renewable energy innovation. The company (1) ________ (establish) in 2015 by a team of environmental scientists who wanted to create clean power from natural sources. Since its beginning, it (2) ________ (design) advanced wave-energy converters to produce electricity from ocean waves. Last year the company (3) ________ (install) these devices along several coastlines to test their efficiency and durability. Its latest product, the AquaGen system, (4) ________ (launch) last month and received excellent feedback from users and energy experts. The system is equipped with smart sensors that monitor wave strength and energy output in real time. Next year, new production facilities (5) ________ (build) in several coastal cities to meet the increasing demand for clean energy.

💡 خطوتين بس وتحلين أي سؤال Active/Passive
  1. الخطوة الأولى — اسألي: مين سوى الفعل؟
    اقرأي الجملة وحطي إصبعك على الفاعل (أول الجملة). اسألي: هل الفاعل هو اللي سوّى الفعل، أو هو اللي انصار عليه الفعل (تلقّاه)؟
    • مثال: "The company ___ (design)" → الشركة هي اللي صممت (هي الفاعل الفعلي) → Active ✅
    • مثال: "The company ___ (establish)" → الشركة ما أسست نفسها، فريق علماء أسسها → Passive ✅ (لازم نضيف was/were + التصريف الثالث)
  2. الخطوة الثانية — حددي الزمن من كلمة بالجملة:
    • in 2015 / last year / last month → زمن ماضي → was/were + V3 (passive) أو V2 (active)
    • Since its beginning → "منذ" يدل على استمرارية من الماضي للحاضر → has/have + V3 (Present Perfect)
    • Next year → المستقبل → will be + V3 (passive) أو will + V1 (active)
  3. قاعدة تركيب الـ Passive دائمًا: be (مصرّف بالزمن المطلوب) + Verb 3 (Past Participle). مثلًا: is/was/will be + built/designed/launched.
#الفعل الأصليالإجابة الصحيحةالزمن المستخدم
1establishwas establishedPassive — Past Simple
2designhas designedActive — Present Perfect
3installinstalledActive — Past Simple
4launchwas launchedPassive — Past Simple
5buildwill be builtPassive — Future Simple
تطبيق القاعدة على كل فعل بالنص
اسأل نفسك: من يقوم بالفعل؟ إذا الفاعل واضح ويقوم بالفعل بنفسه (الشركة صممت، الشركة ركّبت) نستخدم Active. إذا الفاعل (الشيء) يتلقى الفعل ولا يقوم به (الشركة "أُسست" من قبل فريق، النظام "تم إطلاقه") نستخدم Passive. كلمات مثل "last year/last month" = Past Simple، و"next year" = Future، و"since" = Present Perfect.

📖 نص القراءة الكامل — Jobs in the Past and the Future

Jobs in the Past and the Future: The Transformative Role of Technology

A. Work and employment have undergone profound evolutions throughout human history, shaped largely by technological innovation. From the Industrial Revolution to the digital era, technology has continuously redefined how, where, and why people work. Comparing jobs of the past with those likely to exist in the future reveals a pattern of disruption, adaptation, and creation, in which technology acts as both a destroyer of traditional roles and a creator of new opportunities. The rapid development of artificial intelligence (AI), automation, robotics, and digital communication tools will continue to alter the global labour landscape, influencing not only the nature of work but also the skills required to remain employable.

B. Historically, most jobs were physical and routine in nature. During the agricultural and early industrial periods, work relied heavily on manual labour. Farmers, factory workers, and craftsmen formed the backbone of the economy. These roles demanded endurance and craftsmanship rather than cognitive or digital skills. Communication was slow, work was often local, and opportunities for collaboration across borders were limited. Even as the twentieth century introduced mechanisation and assembly lines, workers still needed to perform repetitive tasks, often in fixed locations. Employment was relatively stable, and career progression followed predictable patterns within specific industries.

C. However, the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries witnessed an extraordinary acceleration in technological change. Computers, the internet, and mobile communication reshaped not only business operations but also the expectations of employees and consumers. Jobs that once required face-to-face interaction or manual data entry became automated or digitised. For instance, the rise of word processors replaced typists, while computerised accounting systems reduced the need for clerical staff. At the same time, technology generated new employment sectors—such as software development, information technology support, and digital marketing—that were unimaginable a few decades earlier. These transformations marked a shift from an industrial economy to a knowledge- and service-based economy.

D. Looking forward, technology will continue to revolutionise the world of work, but the direction of change will be even more complex. Artificial intelligence and automation are expected to take over a wide range of repetitive cognitive and physical tasks. Self-driving vehicles may reduce the need for drivers, while AI-powered diagnostic tools could transform healthcare professions. Similarly, robotic process automation will likely replace many back-office roles in finance, logistics, and customer service. As a result, many traditional jobs may disappear or require significant reskilling. According to recent labour studies, up to one-third of existing jobs may be automated by 2030, though the exact impact will vary across regions and sectors.

E. At the same time, the future will bring entirely novel jobs that demand creativity, emotional intelligence, and complex problem-solving—qualities that machines cannot easily replicate. Professions in fields such as data science, cybersecurity, renewable energy, biotechnology, and AI ethics will expand dramatically. Moreover, remote and hybrid work models, supported by digital collaboration tools, will make work more flexible and globalised. The future workforce will not only need technical expertise but also adaptability, critical thinking, and lifelong learning skills to navigate continuous change.

F. Technological change also raises important social and ethical questions. While automation can enhance productivity and reduce human error, it can also increase inequality if opportunities for reskilling and education are not distributed fairly. Workers in low-income or routine jobs are at higher risk of displacement, whereas those with advanced digital skills may benefit the most. Policymakers and educational institutions therefore have a crucial role in ensuring that technological progress leads to inclusive growth. Investment in digital literacy, vocational training, and human-centred design will be essential to balance efficiency with equity. While the future of work will undoubtedly be shaped by automation and artificial intelligence, the human elements such as creativity, empathy, and moral judgment will remain indispensable.

قائمة العناوين المتاحة: I. Work in the past — II. Changing nature of jobs — III. Automation and the future — IV. New skills and opportunities — V. Ethical challenges of technology — VI. The digital shift

  2. دوّري على كلمة مفتاحية متكررة بالفقرة ثم طابقيها بنفس الكلمة أو معناها بالعناوين:
